1.5 Describing copyright and other constraints affecting websites

In regards to constraints there are countless that a website will face. From a development standpoint this includes methods, time, cost, scope, quality, risk and many more confounding variables that can make a negative impact on a website. For example, if not enough time is given for the projects completion the end result can be extremely disappointing due to the cutting corners to reach the deadline. Copyright protects intangible intellectual property; it is separate from the physical work with which it is associated. A person may own the physical painting hanging on her living room wall, but the artist still owns the copyright in the image of that painting. The person owns the physical painting subject to the artist's copyrights, and cannot make posters or note cards from the image of the painting without the artist's permission. In addition to art, you cannot legally duplicate many other common household items, including computer software, movies, and music recordings, without the copyright holder's permission. Posting copyrighted works on file-sharing programs is not only a copyright infringement, it is a crime under the Family Entertainment and Copyright Act of 2005. [6] Therefore, developers have to be very selective over what assets they put onto their websites. This goes for everything from music to images and can land web developers into a copyright lawsuit if ignored. However, developers have tools and methods of getting around this issue by only using materials from open source websites, in which users post their work with the intent of it being widely used. This work is free from copyright as the creator is aware that their work will be made public domain. Alternatively, if an asset you would like to have on your website is copyrighted then you can always attempt to ask the creator for permission to use their work.

1.6 Describing access issues that need taking into account - describe issues that people with disabilities may face when using webpages and how we can overcome them

There are many disabilities and some of them severely impact their ability to use webpages. There are however some functions we can add to offset this. For example, individuals with Parkinson's disease have very unsteady hands which can make clicking on links and buttons a painful experience. To offset this we could make all our links large buttons to minimize the impact on affected individuals. Also when you hover your mouse over an image on a website, the little words that pop up are called alt tags. For someone who has a visual impairment and uses a screen reader (a software program that reads text on a website out loud), the alt tags are read aloud, and are the only way a user knows what the image is. Therefore the use of alt tags is recommended as it is a great way for the visually impaired to understand what an image is. This also extends to putting periods in abbreviations such as CIA and C.I.A as text to speech software will read it out as the word instead of letter by letter as we would read out CIA.
